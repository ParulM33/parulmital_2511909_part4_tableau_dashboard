# Chart Selection Justification
1. Sales Trend (Line Chart) 

This chart answers how sales are changing over time. A line chart is appropriate because it clearly shows trends and patterns across time periods. Order date is used on the x-axis and sales on the y-axis. This avoids confusion and ensures clarity.

 

2. Regional Performance (Bar Chart) 

This chart compares performance across regions. A bar chart is used because it allows easy comparison between different regions. Region is used as the category and sales/profit as values. It avoids clutter and ensures readability.

 

3. Category Profitability (Bar Chart) 

This chart shows how different categories contribute to profit. A bar chart is selected for clear comparison across categories and sub-categories. Color is used to distinguish categories, making patterns easier to identify.

 

4. Customer Segment Analysis (Bar Chart) 

This chart compares performance across customer segments. A bar chart helps easily identify which segment performs better in terms of profit margin. It ensures quick comparison and avoids unnecessary complexity.

 

5. Discount vs Profit (Scatter Plot) 

This chart answers the relationship between discount and profit. A scatter plot is appropriate because it shows correlation between two measures. Discount is plotted on the x-axis and profit on the y-axis.

 

6. Shipping Performance (Bar Chart) 

This chart highlights how shipping delays impact performance. Bar charts are used for clear comparison of shipping modes and delay buckets. It helps identify operational inefficiencies.

 

Design Principles Applied 

- Simple and clean layout 

- Consistent colors 

- Avoided 3D or misleading visuals 

- Used sorting for better readability 

- Focused on business insights rather than decoration 

 

Mistakes Avoided 

- Avoided using pie charts for complex data 

- Avoided cluttered dashboards 

- Avoided inconsistent color usage 

- Avoided misleading scale manipulation
